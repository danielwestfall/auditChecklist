Text Alternatives


This means that if you have anything that isn't text (like pictures, videos, or audio), you need to provide a text version so people who can't see or hear it can still understand it.
Check for:
  • Images: Do all images have "alt text" that describes what the image shows? (Right-click an image and look for "Inspect" or "View Page Source" to see the code, and look for "alt=")
  • Icons: Are icons explained with text or alt text?
  • Videos: Do videos have captions or transcripts?
  • Audio: Do audio files have transcripts?
  • Charts/Graphs: Are charts and graphs explained with text or a text summary?

If you have audio or video files that don't have a visual or sound component, you need to provide a way for people to understand them.
Check for:
  • Audio-only: Does the audio have a transcript or audio description?
  • Video-only: Does the video have audio descriptions or a transcript?

This means the color of the text and the background should be different enough so that people with low vision can read it easily.
Check for:
  • Use a contrast checker tool: Search for "WCAG contrast checker" online. Enter the text and background colors to see if they pass.
  • Large text: Large text (18pt or 14pt bold) needs less contrast.
  • Logos: Logos and brand names are usually exempt.

People should be able to make the text bigger in their browser without the text getting cut off or overlapping.
Check for:
  • Zoom in: Use your browser's zoom feature (usually Ctrl + or Cmd +) to zoom to 200%.
  • Check for overlap: Make sure no text or other content overlaps or disappears.

Images of text can't be resized or read by screen readers as easily as real text.
Check for:
  • Use text: Replace images of text with real text whenever you can.
  • Exceptions: If the text needs to look a certain way (like a logo), an image of text might be okay.
